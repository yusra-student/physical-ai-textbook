Integrating Sensors in Gazebo

In the previous lesson, we learned about the basics of robotic simulation and how to spawn a simple robot in Gazebo. A robot, however, is only as good as its perception of the environment. In this lesson, we will dive into adding and configuring various sensors to our simulated robots in Gazebo, allowing them to "see," "feel," and "understand" their surroundings.

Importance of Sensors in Robotics​

Sensors are the eyes, ears, and touch of a robot. They provide critical information about the robot's internal state and its external environment, which is essential for navigation, manipulation, and interaction. In simulation, we can model a wide array of sensors, allowing us to test perception algorithms without the constraints of physical hardware.

Common Sensor Types in Robotics​

Here are some of the most common sensor types you'll encounter in robotics and how they are typically simulated:

  • LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging): Provides 2D or 3D point cloud data, useful for mapping and obstacle avoidance. Simulated LiDARs emit rays and detect intersections with the environment.
  • Cameras (Monocular, Stereo, Depth): Provide visual information. Simulated cameras render images from the robot's perspective, including RGB, depth, and infrared data.
  • IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit): Measures orientation, angular velocity, and linear acceleration. Essential for robot localization and stabilization. Simulated IMUs provide ideal or noisy sensor readings based on the robot's dynamics.
  • Contact Sensors: Detect physical contact with objects, often used for collision detection.
  • Force/Torque Sensors: Measure forces and torques applied to specific joints or links.

Adding Sensors to Your Robot Model (URDF/XACRO)​

Sensors are typically integrated into your robot's description file (URDF or Xacro). This involves adding new <link> and <joint> elements for the sensor itself and then using Gazebo-specific extensions to define the sensor's properties and how it interacts with the simulation.

Example: Adding a Simple Camera​

Let's consider how you might add a camera to your robot's Xacro file. This involves defining the camera's physical properties and then a Gazebo plugin to simulate its behavior.

<?xml version='1.0'?>
<robot name="simple_robot" xmlns:xacro="http://www.ros.org/wiki/xacro">

  <link name="base_link"/>

  <joint name="camera_joint" type="fixed">
    <parent link="base_link"/>
    <child link="camera_link"/>
    <origin xyz="0.1 0 0.5" rpy="0 0 0"/>
  </joint>

  <link name="camera_link">
    <visual>
      <geometry>
        <box size="0.02 0.05 0.05"/>
      </geometry>
    </visual>
    <collision>
      <geometry>
        <box size="0.02 0.05 0.05"/>
      </geometry>
    </collision>
    <inertial>
      <mass value="0.1"/>
      <inertia ixx="0.001" ixy="0.0" ixz="0.0" iyy="0.001" iyz="0.0" izz="0.001"/>
    </inertial>
  </link>

  <gazebo reference="camera_link">
    <sensor name="camera" type="camera">
      <always_on>true</always_on>
      <update_rate>30.0</update_rate>
      <camera>
        <horizontal_fov>1.047</horizontal_fov>
        <image>
          <width>640</width>
          <height>480</height>
          <format>R8G8B8</format>
        </image>
        <clip>
          <near>0.1</near>
          <far>100</far>
        </clip>
      </camera>
      <plugin name="camera_controller" filename="libgazebo_ros_camera.so">
        <ros>
          <namespace>/camera</namespace>
          <argument>--ros-args --remap __tf:=tf</argument>
        </ros>
        <camera_name>simple_camera</camera_name>
        <frame_name>camera_link_optical</frame_name>
      </plugin>
    </sensor>
  </gazebo>

</robot>

In this example:

  • We define a camera_link and a camera_joint to attach it to the base_link.
  • The <gazebo reference="camera_link"> block contains the Gazebo-specific sensor definition.
  • <sensor name="camera" type="camera"> defines a camera sensor.
  • The libgazebo_ros_camera.so plugin is crucial for bridging the simulated camera data to ROS 2 topics.

Visualizing Sensor Data in ROS 2​

Once your sensors are configured in Gazebo, their data will be published on ROS 2 topics. You can then use tools like RViz to visualize this data.

For a camera, you would typically subscribe to topics like /camera/image_raw or /camera/depth/image_raw. For LiDAR, it might be /scan or /point_cloud.

To visualize camera images in RViz, you would add an Image display and set its topic to your camera's image topic. For LiDAR scans, you would use the LaserScan display.
